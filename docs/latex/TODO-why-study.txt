[Kernel]
- Why study a small kernel: Linux has 30M+ lines; Plan 9's kernel is ~50K.
  You can read the ENTIRE kernel end to end. Understanding the small one
  gives you the mental model to navigate the large one. Every concept in
  Linux (scheduling, memory management, file systems, drivers) exists in
  Plan 9 in a form you can hold in your head.

- Why study an OS at all: most programmers never touch kernel code, but
  every program runs on top of one. Understanding what happens when you
  call read(), malloc(), or fork() — the boundary between your code and
  the machine — changes how you write code above that boundary.

[Compiler]
- Why study a simple compiler: 5c is ~15K lines; GCC is ~15M. The
  compilation pipeline (lex -> parse -> type-check -> IR -> codegen ->
  optimize) is identical. 5c shows each stage in a form small enough to
  understand completely. You can then read GCC/LLVM knowing what each
  phase SHOULD be doing.

- Why study compilation at all: even if you never write a compiler, you
  write code that compilers consume. Understanding what the compiler does
  with your code (inlining, register allocation, dead code elimination)
  makes you write more compiler-friendly code — and more importantly,
  makes you stop writing "clever" code that defeats the optimizer.

[Profiler]
- Why study profiling internals: most developers use profilers as black
  boxes (run perf, look at flamegraph). Understanding HOW sampling works
  (timer interrupts, PC capture, stack unwinding) tells you what the
  profiler can and cannot see — and why it sometimes lies (sampling bias,
  stack truncation, inlined functions disappearing).

[Debugger]
- Why study debugger internals: ptrace, /proc, breakpoint instructions,
  DWARF debug info — these are the mechanisms beneath GDB/LLDB. When the
  debugger does something unexpected (can't set a breakpoint in optimized
  code, shows wrong variable value, can't unwind past a signal handler),
  knowing the mechanism explains why.

- Why study Plan 9's acid vs GDB: acid is a programmable debugger — it's
  a language, not a fixed tool. This is the difference between a debugger
  you USE and a debugger you EXTEND. The idea reappears in rr's scripting,
  LLDB's Python API, and eBPF-based observability.

[Linker]
- Why study object file formats: they're the contract between compiler and
  linker, between build time and load time. Understanding ELF/a.out explains
  why certain build errors happen, why symbol visibility matters, why
  static and dynamic linking have different trade-offs.

[Assembler]
- Why study an assembler when compilers generate assembly: reading assembly
  output (gcc -S, objdump -d) is the only way to verify what the compiler
  actually did. Performance debugging frequently bottoms out at "what
  instructions did it emit?" Understanding the assembler's job makes
  assembly output readable.

[Machine]
- Why study through an emulator: you can single-step, inspect every
  register, watch every memory access. On real hardware these operations
  are mediated by a debugger; in an emulator they're first-class. The
  emulator IS the textbook — it's the spec made executable.

- Why study ARM specifically: ARM won mobile (every phone has one), is
  winning laptops (Apple M-series), and is entering servers (AWS Graviton).
  RISC-V follows the same principles. x86 internally decodes to RISC
  micro-ops. Understanding one RISC ISA transfers to all of them.

[Shell]
- Why study rc instead of bash: rc is ~2K lines; bash is ~140K. rc
  implements the same core concepts (pipes, redirections, variables,
  control flow, job control) in 1/70th the code. Once you understand
  rc, bash's extra features are incremental additions, not new concepts.

- Why study shell internals at all: every CI/CD pipeline, every Dockerfile,
  every Makefile recipe runs through a shell. When your build breaks in
  mysterious ways (quoting, word splitting, glob expansion, subshell
  variable scope), the shell's internals explain why.

[Utilities]
- Why study cat/grep/sort internals: they look trivial from outside, but
  they encode fundamental patterns (filter, transform, reduce) that appear
  in every data pipeline. Understanding how sort handles files larger than
  memory (external merge sort) or how grep compiles a regex to a DFA
  transfers to any system that processes data.

- Why study Plan 9 utilities vs GNU coreutils: Plan 9 versions are
  typically 10-50x smaller. The feature delta (what GNU added) is itself
  educational — it shows which features were driven by real need vs.
  accretion. Many GNU additions exist for backward compatibility with
  quirks nobody remembers.

[Windows]
- Why study a window system: every GUI application sits on top of one.
  Understanding the display server / compositor / window manager split
  explains why Wayland behaves differently from X11, why screen sharing
  is hard, why games bypass the compositor. The concepts map directly
  to mobile (Android SurfaceFlinger, iOS UIKit).

[Lib_graphics]
- Why study a 2D graphics library: every pixel on your screen was put
  there by code like this. Modern GPUs accelerate it, but the operations
  (bitblt, clipping, compositing, alpha blending) are unchanged. Plan 9's
  draw library influenced Go's image package — the API is nearly identical.

[Lib_gui]
- Why study a widget library: every GUI framework (Qt, GTK, SwiftUI,
  React) solves the same problems: widget trees, layout, event dispatch,
  painting. libpanel does it in ~3K lines. Understanding the simple
  version makes the complex ones (with accessibility, theming, animation,
  GPU rendering) comprehensible — you see what's essential vs. what's
  added complexity.

[Lib_networking]
- Why study a network stack: every web request traverses one. Understanding
  the path from write() through TCP segmentation, IP routing, and Ethernet
  framing to the NIC explains latency, explains why TCP_NODELAY exists,
  explains what happens when packets are lost. Cloud networking (VPCs,
  load balancers, service meshes) is this stack virtualized.

[Lib_core]
- Why study malloc internals: every non-trivial program allocates memory.
  Understanding fragmentation, arena allocation, and free-list strategies
  explains why your program's RSS grows but never shrinks, why jemalloc
  and tcmalloc exist, and why Rust's ownership model is such a big deal.

- Why study string/unicode internals: every program processes text.
  Understanding UTF-8 encoding, rune iteration, and the difference between
  bytes and characters prevents an entire class of bugs (truncating in
  the middle of a multi-byte character, comparing bytes vs. code points,
  locale-dependent sorting).

[Filesystem]
- Why study a filesystem: every program reads and writes files. The
  filesystem is where the abstraction of "a file" meets the reality of
  "a spinning disk" or "flash cells with wear leveling." Understanding
  this boundary explains fsync semantics, why rename() is atomic but
  write() isn't, and why databases don't trust the filesystem.

[Editors]
- Why study an editor's internals: you spend more time in an editor than
  any other program. Understanding gap buffers vs. rope data structures,
  syntax highlighting via incremental parsing, and undo as an operation
  log gives you vocabulary to evaluate editors and extend them. Plan 9's
  sam pioneered structural regular expressions — an idea still ahead of
  most editors.

[Formatters]
- Why study a text formatter: TeX/troff are the invisible infrastructure
  behind most technical documents. Understanding line-breaking algorithms
  (Knuth-Plass), font metrics, and page layout is understanding the
  machinery that produces every PDF you read. troff's pipeline model
  (preprocessors for tables, equations, pictures piped into the formatter)
  is the Unix philosophy applied to document production.

[Auth]
- Why study authentication internals: every networked system authenticates.
  Understanding challenge-response protocols, ticket-based auth (Kerberos,
  Plan 9's factotum), and capability delegation explains why OAuth exists,
  why JWT has the security properties it does, and why "just use passwords"
  is never enough.
