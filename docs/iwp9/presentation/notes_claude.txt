%claude:                                                                                 
  Books as a form:
  - Printed books: ~580 years (Gutenberg, ~1440s)                                     
  - Codex (bound pages): ~2000 years (replaced scrolls, 1st-4th century AD)
  - Written teaching texts: ~4000+ years (Egyptian, Mesopotamian)
  - Books have survived every "this will kill books" technology: radio, TV, internet,
  video courses, MOOCs

  Why books are still the gold standard for deep technical teaching:
  - Curated narrative: the author chose the order, the pacing, what to show first and
  what to defer — exactly what literate programming is about
  - Deep focus: sustained attention, unlike browsing code in an IDE or watching videos
  - Completeness: no gaps, everything is covered
  - Referenceable: you can flip back, search, study at your own pace — videos can't do
   this well
  - Proven: every field's foundational knowledge lives in books (Knuth's TAOCP,
  Feynman's Lectures, Euclid's Elements)

  The alternatives fall short for THIS purpose:
  - IDE/code browsing: shows code in compiler order, not understanding order. No
  narrative, no "why". You see everything at once — overwhelming.
  - Video courses: linear but can't be referenced, searched, or studied at your own
  pace
  - AI on-demand explanation: no coherent narrative, no careful separation of
  concerns, no curated ordering. Ask Claude to "explain the Plan 9 kernel" and you get
   something generic. A literate program gives you the author's deeply considered
  presentation order, refined over years.
  - Interactive notebooks: great for learning-by-doing, not for understanding an
  existing 35K LOC kernel

  Syncweb brings the best of IDEs into books:
  - Cross-references with hyperlinks = like "go to definition" in an IDE
  - Evince inline thumbnails = like hover-preview in VS Code
  - But with the narrative and ordering that an IDE can never provide
  - Best of both worlds
